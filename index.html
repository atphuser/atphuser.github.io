    Frequently Asked Questions

    How long will it take me to see results?
    Joseph Pilates said “In 10 sessions, you will feel better, in 20 sessions you look better, 30 sessions you have a completely new body.” Consistency is key. With regular practice (1-3 times per week), your body will begin to feel different (relief of aches, pain, and muscle tension) in just a few weeks. Soon after, you will notice an improvement in your posture and muscle tone. These effects will only continue to develop with time and consistency, making Pilates a life-long habit rather than a means to an end.
    Should I take mat classes, duets, or private sessions?

    Many Pilates practitioners like to mix it up with a combination of private sessions, group classes, and/or duets. If you are new to Pilates, we recommend private sessions to start. This way, you can get to know the equipment in a one-on-one environment that is catered to you and to any injuries or concerns you might have. If you are pre-or post-natal, we have great class and private options to fit your needs. Both Morwen and Risa have training and experience with prenatal Pilates and can guide you safely through your pregnancy. Still unsure? Just ask! We are happy to help you find the combination that best works for you.

    What should I wear?
    Wear comfortable clothing that you can move in.

    Pilates 101

    What is Pilates?
    Pilates is a full-body strength-training program that emphasizes core strength, flexibility, breath, and mind-body awareness. Emphasis on the body's core, or Powerhouse, creates increased stability of the entire body. The many benefits of a regular Pilates practice include injury prevention, beautiful posture, long, lean muscle tone, and relief from back pain.
    Power-House Pilates is an authentic Pilates studio. We teach Pilates as Joseph Pilates intended, using specific exercises on authentic equipment. We do this because we know that these exercises, performed in the order that he designed, will strengthen and balance the body and mind in a way that is invigorating, interesting, and timeless enough to maintain throughout a person's life.
    Who Was Joseph Pilates?
    Born in 1883 in Germany, Joseph Pilates was unhealthy and frail as a child. In order to improve his health, Pilates studied anatomy and became skilled at wrestling, boxing, and bodybuilding, eventually finding a career as a boxer and circus performer. Forced into an internment camp in England during WWI, he developed resistance-based rehabilitation exercises for detainees who were injured and unwell. These exercises were the beginnings of the Pilates method, which Joseph Pilates would develop fully after his arrival in New York in 1925. Inventing equipment and continuing to refine the method, Pilates and his wife, Clara, taught students and trained teachers together in their New York City studio for forty years. They found a special niche among dancers and athletes, who valued the Pilates method for its strength- building and rehabilitative effects. Joseph Pilates died in 1967, and Clara continued to teach the method for another ten years.
    The Six Principles of Pilates
    • Centering: The initiation of all movement from the body's center, or Powerhouse, which allows for the development of muscles throughout the whole body without unnecessary stress and strain.
    • Concentration: The ability to focus all of one's awareness on the exercise and muscles at hand, thus establishing a mind-body connection.
    • Control: Performing all exercises with total control over one's muscles. In Pilates, control is more important than speed and repetition in producing results.
    • Precision: When performing the exercises, proper alignment and placement of the body are important for safety and maximum results.
    • Breath: Breath is a very important aspect of Pilates. Most exercises are coordinated with the breath, which helps with concentration and full engagement of the muscles.
    • Flow: Grace and fluidity are cornerstones of the Pilates method, and should be goals for each exercise. In an ideal Pilates practice, each movement and exercise should flow from one to the next with breath and physical ease.
